Crown & Conjure

A prompt builder for Black hair in fantasy worlds.

AI image tools default to Eurofantasy. Black users do extra prompt labor just to get accurate hair textures and styles—and even then, results often flatten or stereotype. This is a small intervention: a curated dictionary of Black hairstyles paired with fantasy archetype prompts, formatted for the tool you're using. Built in the spirit of A.M. Darke's Open Source Afro Hair Library and the Dove + OSAHL Code My Crown guide.

Why this exists

AI image generators are trained on the open web, which means they inherit its biases. Default outputs skew white, straight-haired, and Eurofantasy. To get Black characters with accurate hair, users have to add qualifiers, name specific styles, fight the model's defaults, and often regenerate many times. This is unpaid labor—the cost of being a non-default user.

Sociologist Ruha Benjamin calls this kind of pattern "the New Jim Code": new technologies that "reflect and reproduce existing inequities but that are promoted and perceived as more objective and progressive than the discriminatory systems of a previous era." AI image gen presents itself as neutral—the algorithm doesn't see race—but the asset web it was trained on already encodes a hierarchy in which white European aesthetics are unmarked and centered. The bias is the inheritance, not the intent.

Fantasy is one of the worst-affected genres. The Tolkien-derived high fantasy that underwrites most game art treats whiteness as the unmarked default. Hogwarts Legacy, Dragon Age, Skyrim, Elden Ring—all have been criticized for poor Black hair options in their character creators. In her 2013 essay "How Long 'til Black Future Month?", N.K. Jemisin asks the question that drives this project: where are the Black people in our imagined futures? Meanwhile, an entire tradition of Black fantasy literature—Jemisin, Tomi Adeyemi, Nnedi Okorafor—imagines fantasy worlds where Black aesthetics are central, not exotic.

This tool is a small attempt to lower the prompt labor cost. It encodes the vocabulary and cultural specificity that experienced Black AI users have figured out through trial and error, so newcomers don't have to start from zero.

Lineage

This project stands on the shoulders of:

  • A.M. Darke's Open Source Afro Hair Library—free 3D models of Black hair, made by Black artists, addressing the asset-store representation crisis. Darke documented that searches for "Black hair" on TurboSquid returned literal horses, racist caricatures, and hypersexualized imagery before this project existed.
  • Code My Crown—Dove + OSAHL's free 200-page guide for coding textured Black hair in games, with 15 hair sculpts and cultural context. Many style descriptions in this dictionary draw on Code My Crown's framing.
  • Amira Virgil (Xmiramira)'s Melanin Pack and The Black Simmer community—a parallel modding infrastructure built because EA wouldn't. Recent academic work frames The Black Simmer as a "site of theoretical praxis" centered on mentoring, collective care, and Black joy (Smith et al., "Constructing a Community of Play," Proc. ACM HCI, 2025).
  • Nnedi Okorafor's framing of Africanfuturism—distinct from Afrofuturism, rooted in continental African cultures and futures rather than diaspora.
  • Woodrow Winchester's argument for Afrofuturism as a "design lens" that makes biases visible and provides "a methodological framework for challenging and addressing them."

Prompt anti-patterns

Words that hurt more than help when prompting AI:

dreadlocks→ locs (the "dread" prefix has colonial roots)
ethnic / exotic→ name the specific origin or culture
tribal→ name the actual tradition (Yoruba, Maasai, Akan, etc.)
nappy / kinky→ type 4 hair, coily texture, natural texture
African braids→ specify: Fulani, Senegalese, box, cornrows, etc.
dark-skinned (alone)→ deep brown / rich brown / warm brown skin

A note on authority

This is a starting point, not a definitive reference. Hairstyle origins and cultural context deserve careful sourcing—the descriptions here are deliberately brief and should be cross-checked against community sources (Code My Crown, Ayana Byrd & Lori Tharps's Hair Story: Untangling the Roots of Black Hair in America, and the work of A.M. Darke). Corrections and additions welcome via the GitHub repo.

How to use the prompts

Copy a prompt, paste it into your tool of choice (Midjourney via Discord, DALL-E in ChatGPT, or any Stable Diffusion / Flux interface). Generate at least 4 outputs—AI is stochastic, and the first result rarely captures cultural specificity. Iterate by adding details that worked and removing ones that didn't.
